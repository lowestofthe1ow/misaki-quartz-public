Proposition §

A proposition is a declarative sentence that is either true or false, but not both. All the following declarative sentences are propositions; propositions 1 and 3 are true, whereas 2 and 4 are false.

  1. Washington, D.C., is the capital of the United States of America.E
  2. Toronto is the capital of Canada.
  3. 1 + 1 = 2.
  4. 2 + 2 = 3.

Propositional variables §

We use letters to denote propositional variables (or sentential variables), that is, variables that represent propositions, just as letters are used to denote numerical variables.

When declaring propositional variables, use simple (atomic) propositions. Avoid operators in declarations.

Truth value §

The truth value of a proposition is true, denoted by T, if it is a true proposition, and false, denoted by F, if it is a false proposition.

Compound propositions §

Formed from existing propositions using logical operators

Logical operators §

Operators that take existing propositions to form compound propositions

Truth table §

A table consisting of n input columns, an output column, and xn rows, where x is the number of possible values (true or false means 2 values) and n is the number of variables

Negation (NOT) §

  • ¬p (read as “not p“)
  • Unary operator (takes only one operand)
  • ¬p is translated as “It is not the case that p”
  • Example:
    • p: “I sleep early”
    • ¬p: “I do not sleep early”

Conjunction (AND) §

  • p∧q (read as ”p and q“)
  • Binary operator (takes two operands)
  • True if and only if p and q are both true
  • Example:
    • p: “I will sleep early tonight”
    • q: “I will eat breakfast tomorrow morning”
    • p∧q: “I will sleep early tonight and eat breakfast tomorrow morning”

Disjunction (Inclusive OR) §

  • p∨q (read as ”p or q“)
  • Binary operator (takes two operands)
  • True if at least one operand is true
  • Example:
    • p: “I wake up early”
    • q: “I am late for class”
    • p∨q: “I wake up early or I am late for class”

Exclusive or (XOR) §

  • p⊕q (read as ”p xor q“)
  • Binary operator (takes two operands)
  • True if either p or q is true, but not both (i.e., p and q must have different truth values)
  • Example:
    • p: “The steak comes with soup”
    • q: “The steak comes with dessert”
    • p⊕q: “The steak comes with soup or dessert, but not both”

Assume inclusive or unless stated otherwise.

Implication (conditional) §

  • p→q (read as ”p implies q”, or “if p then q“)
  • p is the premise, q is the consequence
    • If the premise happened, then so will the consequence.
  • In other words:
    • p is sufficient for q
    • q is necessary for p
    • q whenever p

Converse §

  • The converse of p→q is q→p
  • Equivalent to the inverse

Inverse §

  • The inverse of p→q is ¬p→¬q
  • Equivalent to the converse

Contrapositive §

  • The contrapositive of p→q is ¬q→¬p
  • Equivalent to the original statement

Biconditional (IFF) §

  • p↔q (read as ”p if and only if q” or ”p is sufficient and necessary for q“)
  • True if p→q and q→p are both true (i.e., p and q have the same truth value)
